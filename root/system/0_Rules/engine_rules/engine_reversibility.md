# Engine Reversibility Rules

The engine is a mechanical layer.  
It does not interpret, evaluate, or assign meaning.  
Reversibility ensures that engine operations leave no residue and create no implicit state.

## 1. Engine operations must be mechanically reversible
Every mechanical action performed by the engine must be undoable.  
Reversibility is structural, not semantic.

## 2. The engine does not create irreversible transformations
The engine cannot:
- compress content,
- expand content,
- reinterpret content,
- mutate structure in a way that cannot be undone.

It only moves content along coordinates.

## 3. The engine leaves no residue
After completing its mechanical operation, the engine must leave behind:
- no partial structures,
- no hidden state,
- no intermediate artifacts,
- no lingering positional bindings.

Nothing remains once the operation ends.

## 4. The engine does not accumulate state
The engine cannot store:
- previous positions,
- previous coordinate paths,
- previous objects,
- previous content.

Reversibility requires that nothing persists.

## 5. The engine does not depend on history
The engine cannot rely on:
- prior turns,
- prior movements,
- prior coordinate sequences,
- prior placements.

Each mechanical operation is independent.

## 6. The engine does not alter upstream or downstream structures
Reversibility requires that the engine cannot:
- modify coordinate systems,
- redefine positional schemas,
- introduce new structural forms,
- alter domain boundaries.

It only moves content mechanically.

## 7. The engine discards all content at the end of its operation
Once the engine completes its movement:
- all content is removed,
- all positional bindings are cleared,
- all mechanical state is reset.

Nothing persists.

## 8. Nothing fuses
Reversibility requires that no domain, content, or structure merges with another.  
The engine remains isolated and mechanically clean.
